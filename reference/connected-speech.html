Connected Speech — Quick Reference

Why English sounds "fast": words are not said one at a time. They link, weaken, and drop sounds so a sentence becomes one smooth ribbon. Master these five features and your speech (and listening) jumps a level.

Phonetics use IPA. /…/ marks a sound; the · shows a linked join; strikethrough shows a dropped sound.

The five features

FeatureWhat happensExample
1 · Consonant → Vowel
辅音 + 元音		 Final consonant hitches onto the next vowel. The single most common link. an apple → a·napple /əˈnæpl/
pick it up → pi·ki·tup
2 · Consonant → Consonant
辅音 + 辅音		 Same/similar consonants merge into one long sound; sometimes a sound drops (elision). bad day → ba(d)-day
next day → ne(k)s(t)-day (elision 吞音)
3 · Vowel → Vowel
元音 + 元音		 A tiny /w/, /j/ or /r/ slides in between so the mouth can move smoothly. go away → go·w·away /w/
he is → he·j·is /j/
far away → far·r·away /r/
4 · Weak forms
弱读		 Grammar words (a, of, to, and, for…) shrink to the schwa /ə/ when unstressed. cup of tea → cup-/ə/-tea
"of" is rarely /ɒv/ — it's /ə/
5 · Stress & thought groups
重音 + 意群		 Linking happens inside a thought group; tiny pauses fall between groups. Content words are stressed, grammar words are not. I'll be there ‖ in about ten minutes.

The golden rule

Remember: Link inside a thought group, pause between thought groups. Never link across a comma, a breath, or a meaning boundary — that's what makes speech sound slurred rather than fluent.

The practice loop

  1. Listen to a natural-speed model (a single sentence).
  2. Mark the links with a pencil — where does one word run into the next?
  3. Shadow: say it with the audio, matching the links, 3× in a row.
  4. Record yourself on the phone once a week and compare. The gap you hear is the gap to close.
