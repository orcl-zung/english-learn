# English Pronunciation Glossary

Terms for connected speech & rhythm. Grammar terms will be added in Phase 2.

## Connected speech

**Connected speech (连读 / 连音):**
The way words join in natural, fluent speech so a sentence flows as one stream of sound rather than isolated words.
_Avoid:_ "fast speech", "slurring"

**Linking (连读):**
Connecting the final sound of one word to the first sound of the next so there is no gap between them.
_Avoid:_ blending (reserve for when two sounds merge into one)

**Consonant-to-vowel linking:**
When a word ends in a consonant sound and the next begins with a vowel sound, the consonant hitches a ride onto the vowel: *an apple* → *a-napple*.

**Glide (/w/ or /j/):**
A short on-glide inserted between two vowel sounds so the mouth can move smoothly: round-lip vowel → /w/ (*go·w·away*), spread vowel → /j/ (*he·j·is*).

**Linking /r/ (BrE):**
A normally-silent word-final /r/ (e.g. in *car*, *four*, *there*) that reappears when the next word begins with a vowel: *far away* → *far·r·away*. The single most recognisable marker of British English.

**Intrusive /r/:**
A /r/ inserted after a word ending in /ə/ or /ɔː/ even when there's no *r* in the spelling: *idea of* → *idea·r·of*, *law and order* → *law·r·and*. Common in BrE; considered non-standard but ubiquitous.

**Elision (吞音):**
A sound disappears in fluent speech, usually /t/ or /d/ caught between consonants: *next day* → *ne(k)s day*.

**Gemination (相同辅音长读):**
When one word ends and the next begins with the *same* consonant, the two merge into one sustained, lengthened sound — not two separate taps: *bad dog* → one long /d/.

**Yod-coalescence (融合):**
/t/ or /d/ meeting a following /j/ fuses into /tʃ/ or /dʒ/: *don't you* → *donchoo*, *did you* → *didja*.

**Glottal stop /ʔ/ (喉塞音):**
A catch of breath in the throat instead of a crisp release. In BrE, word-final /t/ before another consonant often becomes one: *water* /wɔːʔə/, *cat flap* /kæʔ flæp/.

**Schwa /ə/:**
The reduced, relaxed vowel — the most common vowel in English. It is why unstressed syllables sound so short and vague: *about*, the second syllable of *photograph*.

**Weak form:**
The reduced pronunciation of a function word in connected speech, usually centring on the schwa: *can* /kæn/ → /kən/, *of* /ɒv/ → /ə/. The default wherever the word is unstressed.

**Strong form:**
The full, clear pronunciation of a word — used when stressed, isolated, or emphasised. Function words switch between strong and weak depending on stress.

**Stress-timed:**
A rhythm where stressed syllables recur at roughly equal intervals and unstressed syllables compress to fit. English is stress-timed; Chinese is syllable-timed (each syllable near-equal weight — the source of the "robotic" accent).

**Thought group (意群):**
A short stretch of speech said as one unit, with a tiny pause at each end. Linking happens *inside* a thought group, never across its pauses.

**Sentence stress:**
The emphasis placed on certain words within a thought group — achieved by higher pitch, greater length, and loudness. Usually one primary (focal) stress per group; shifting it changes the meaning.

**Content word:**
Words that carry meaning and get stressed: nouns, main verbs, adjectives, adverbs, question words, demonstratives.

**Function word:**
Grammar words that get reduced to weak forms: articles, prepositions, auxiliaries, pronouns, conjunctions.
